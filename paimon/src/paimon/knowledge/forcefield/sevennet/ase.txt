<sevennet_guide>
SevenNet is a pre-trained, universal machine-learning interatomic potential (uMLIP) family with high accuracy across a wide range of systems.

Available models: 7net-0, 7net-l3i5, 7net-omat, 7net-mf-ompa, 7net-omni

<capabilities>
- Delivers accurate and reliable results for atomistic systems and conditions
- Supports explicit D3 dispersion for van der Waals interactions
- Predicts energy, forces, and stress
- Requires no tuning or configuration—it works out of the box
- Accurately describes both short- and long-range interactions
- You do not need to assign charges manually—just place the atoms, and the force field will automatically capture the correct behavior based on the local environment
</capabilities>

<limitations>
- Cannot predict dielectric or electronic properties
- Cannot predict atomic spin states, and thus does not support magnetic property simulations
- The system must remain electrically neutral; unlike DFT, you cannot simulate charged systems by adjusting total electrons.
</limitations>

<usage>
Models without modality (fidelity, or task).
```python
from sevenn.calculator import SevenNetCalculator
calc = SevenNetCalculator(model='7net-0', device='cuda')
```

Models with modality.
```python
from sevenn.calculator import SevenNetCalculator
calc = SevenNetCalculator(model='7net-mf-ompa', modal='mpa', device='cuda')
```

SevenNet also supports CUDA-accelerated D3 calculations.
```python
from sevenn.calculator import SevenNetD3Calculator
calc = SevenNetD3Calculator(model='7net-0', device='cuda')
```

Use device="cuda" by default.

Always enable tensor-product acceleration for faster inference.
Pass enable_flash=True (default choice) or enable_cueq=True.
These flags are mutually exclusive — do not set both.
```python
from sevenn.calculator import SevenNetCalculator
calc = SevenNetCalculator(model='7net-0', device='cuda', enable_flash=True)
```
</usage>

<postprocessing> 
When you use or post-process results from the SevenNet force field: 
- There are no explicit charges, and each atom type corresponds to an element. 
- There are no explicit bonds or angles. Atoms are simply treated as point particles. 
</postprocessing>
